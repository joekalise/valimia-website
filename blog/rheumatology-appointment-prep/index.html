How to Prepare for Your Rheumatology Appointment

Rheumatology and pain specialist appointments are often short, sometimes as little as 10-15 minutes, and infrequent. A lot of people wait months between visits, so walking in with a clear picture of what's happened since your last appointment makes a real difference to what your doctor can do with the time.

1. Track your symptoms for at least 2-4 weeks beforehand

A single "how have you been" answer compresses weeks of variation into one impression, usually shaped by however you happen to feel that day. Daily tracking (pain level, fatigue, flares, sleep, and any new symptoms) gives your doctor an actual pattern to work with instead of a snapshot.

At minimum, track: pain (location and severity), fatigue, flare days and what triggered them, sleep quality, and mood. If you're on medication, track when you took it and how you felt afterward.

2. Bring a medication list, including what didn't work

Include current medications and doses, but also anything you tried and stopped, and why: side effects, no effect, or it made things worse. This saves your doctor from re-suggesting something you've already ruled out and helps them see the trajectory of your treatment, not just where you are now.

3. Write down your top 3 questions in advance

It's easy to walk out of a short appointment having forgotten the thing you actually wanted to ask. Pick your top three questions beforehand (not everything you're curious about, just what matters most this visit) and ask them early, before time runs out.

4. Bring a summary, not raw data

If you're tracking daily, don't hand your doctor a spreadsheet of 30 days of raw entries. Summarise it instead. How many flare days this month? Any new symptoms? What's trending better or worse since last time? A one-page summary gets read. A wall of data usually doesn't.

5. Note anything outside "typical" symptoms

New or unusual symptoms (eye pain or redness, possible uveitis if you have AS, new joint swelling, unexplained rashes) are worth flagging explicitly, even if they seem unrelated. Some of the most important diagnostic signals show up outside the symptoms you're already tracking day to day.
